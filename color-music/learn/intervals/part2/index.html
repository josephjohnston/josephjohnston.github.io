How to Use

Click on any small circle inside either of the two big circles to see the interval relationship that particular note holds with C. We will call the note you click on the 'focus' note. Move around the circle on the left in the clockwise direction to read the tutorials below in the order of the circle of fifths. The circle of fifths is displayed on the left so that you can see the relationship of the focus note to C in terms of their colors. The chromatic circle is displayed on the right so you can see the relationship of the focus note and C in terms of their relative position on the piano. Scroll down to see details regarding the string length and frequency of the focus note relative to bottom C (the lowest string). Continue scrolling and you will see two ‘blocks’ of frequencies illustrating sound waves. Examine these blocks closely. The less often the two frequencies in a block meet together at the top of their wave, the more complex their mathematical relationship and thus the more dissonant the interval. The block on the left shows the frequency of the focus note layered on top of the frequency of the bottom C (the lowest string). The block on the right shows the frequency of the focus note layered on top of the frequency of the top C (the highest string). We include this second block regarding the top string because, as you will see on the keyboard below the sound waves, the focus note not only forms an interval with the C below it, but also with the C above it. This other interval formed by the focus note and the top C is the 'reciprocal' of the interval formed by the focus note and the bottom C. Reciprocals will be discussed on this page. As you probably sense, this section is complex. It is aimed at demonstrating the mathematical precision of how notes of different colors form different intervals with different degrees of harmony and dissonance.
