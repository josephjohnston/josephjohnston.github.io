What is a Scale?

A scale is a group of related notes organized to shape melodies and harmony in songs. If you play or improvise on the piano disregarding any use for a scale, you are essentially randomizing your notes. Any standard song is built upon a scale of some sort. Each scale’s different collection of notes renders a different mood. Some scales sound happy, some sad, and some awkward or eerie. The standard scale we will discuss, which is called the ‘diatonic’ scale, derives from a simple set of complementary notes. On this page, we will identify the structure of the diatonic scale. Let’s begin. If you recall, notes located near each other on the circle of fifths complement each other harmoniously. Therefore, in order to concoct a set of complementary notes to form the diatonic scale, we will simply choose a series of neighboring notes from the circle of fifths. More specifically, we will select the seven notes you see at the top of the circle below. These seven notes will form the top row of the table graphic shown below the circle.

The Diatonic Scale  |  Show

If you try playing the seven notes of this top row sequentially, your hands will jump sporadically up and down the keyboard. Scales do not hop around like this, but instead use a linear format that moves in a single direction on the keyboard. In order to morph these notes into the diatonic scale, we must formulate the notes in a linear arrangement. See the sketched table at the left to see this scale configuration in its desired format. If you look closely, you’ll see that we constructed the top row of notes from left to right following the order of notes in the circle of fifths. In the second row we slid each note one position to the left. This repositioning process continues until every note appears in every possible position within a row. When we take the diagonal line of notes from top left to bottom right, you might notice something interesting. Click ‘show’ to see it. What’s so special about the notes in this diagonal line? They make up the order of notes in the diatonic scale! Unlike the circle of fifths, the diatonic scale skips every other note in the top row. In other words, we can reach the order of notes in the diatonic scale by taking every other note from any seven sequential notes in the circle of fifths.

Major and Minor Scales  |  Show

Now that we know how to reach the diatonic scale, let’s look at another table. This time we will use the order of notes in the diatonic scale as the top row, as shown to the right. Instead of looking at the diagonal order this time, we will focus on the horizontal rows. Again, the general order of notes in each row is referred to as the diatonic scale. You can construct a diatonic scale with any group of seven adjacent notes on the circle of fifths. Because each row starts with a different note, each of these seven rows show a different version of the diatonic scale. These different versions are called ‘modes.’ Two of these modes stand out among the rest for their fulfilling sound. Click ‘show’ to see these two modes highlighted. The mode beginning with A is called the minor scale and the one below, which begins with C, is called the major scale. The minor and major scales can be found in almost every melody you’ve ever heard. Play them and you’ll notice that the major scale sounds happy and confident, while the minor scale seems downcast and despondent. Anything you play will be named after the note you start with and the mode you choose. For example, you might play a song using the ‘C major’ scale. You can also say that you are playing in the ‘key of C major.’

Procedure

We hope you now understand the origin of the major and minor scale. To review, here’s the procedure:
 1. Choose seven neighboring notes from the circle of fifths.
 2. Select every other note from that series.
 3. Now with a new array of notes, re-order the array by taking the note of the left and attaching it to the end of the               right side until one of the following two scenarios are met.
       A. The third note in the array appears on the left side, turning the arrangement into the minor scale.
       B. The fifth note in the array appears on the left side, turning the arrangement into the major scale. 

We have provided these tables, rows, diagonal lines, and constant rearrangement to explain the origin of the two scales, major and minor, that you will use every time you play. Of course there are more efficient methods of identifying these scales. For example, you can start on any note and then follow the interval sequence for the major or minor scale as illustrated below
('M' denotes a major interval, and 'm' denotes a minor interval.)
