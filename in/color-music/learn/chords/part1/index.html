What is a Chord?  |  Show

A chord is the combination of three or more notes played simultaneously for their cohesive sound. Which notes? Let’s take another look at that second table graphic (the major and minor scales) we examined in Scales Part 1, shown here on the left. Click ‘show’ to highlight a diagonal line from top left to bottom right. If you remember, this diagonal line features every other note of the top row. In other words, we are looking at every other note of a mode on the diatonic scale. That’s what a chord is – every other note of the diatonic scale!

Major and Minor Thirds  |  Show

To see the chords for the major and minor modes, we need to refer to the table to the right. We built this table using the same conversion procedure we did in Scales Part 1. Click ‘show’ to reveal which rows feature the chords for the major and minor scale. But what exactly are these chords? If you examine the notes of the minor scale chord (top row), you will notice A, C, E... from left to right. This sequence of notes has a structure that we can examine by breaking the sequence into intervals. A and C form a minor third interval. C and E form a major third interval. Notice that the remainder of the row consists of alternating major and minor thirds, but that the D and F minor third interrupts the pattern. This interval results in two consecutive minor thirds. Now, scan the major scale chord row and you’ll observe a similar pattern. The difference here is that the row starts with a major third instead of a minor third. Examine the remaining rows and you’ll see more rows that begin with major and minor thirds and that contain the double minor third.

Structure

We now know that chords are built by stacking major and minor thirds. There are a number of chord types that differ depending on how they arrange these two intervals. Following, I will describe the seven most common chords used in music (again, 'M' denotes a major interval, and 'm' denotes a minor interval).

Major Triad

The major triad is composed of a major third on the bottom and a minor third on the top. It is called a ‘triad’ because it comprises three notes. Play it and hear its cheerful harmony. Notice that the major triad is made up of the first three notes of the major scale chord row.

C M3 E m3 G

Minor Triad

The minor triad is composed of a minor third on the bottom and a major third on the top. This chord has a darker sound than the major triad. Notice that the minor triad is made up of the first three notes of the minor scale chord row. Try to locate all the major and minor triads in the second table graphic above.

A m3 C M3 E

Major Seventh

The major seventh is created when you take the major triad and stack another major third on top. It is called the major seventh chord because the note added to the top produces a major seventh interval with the bottom note. Notice that the major seventh chord consists of the first four notes of the major scale chord row.

C M3 E m3 G M3 B

Minor Seventh

You get the minor seventh chord when you take the minor triad and stack another minor third on the top. It is called the minor seventh chord because that last note added on the top produces a minor seventh interval with the bottom note in the chord. Notice that the major seventh chord is made up of the first four notes of the major scale chord row.

A m3 C M3 E m3 G

Dominant Seventh

The dominant seventh chord takes advantage of the double minor third to render a clear, resonating sound. This chord can be heard when you add another minor third to the top of a major triad. By now, we are no longer simply alternating major and minor thirds. Notice that the first four notes of the fifth chord row make up a dominant seventh chord.

G M3 B m3 D m3 F

Diminished Triad

The diminished triad also depends on the double minor third. Simply stacking two minor thirds will give you a diminished triad. This chord sounds compressed, much like the whistle of a train. Notice that the diminished triad appears in every row except the top and bottom rows.

B m3 D m3 F

Augmented Triad

This last chord does not appear in the scale chord rows shown above because it does not exist in the diatonic scale. We are including it for its simple structure and unique sound. You’ll hear the augmented triad when you play two stacked major thirds. Notice its open, floating resonance.

C M3 E M3 Ab

Inversions

Other chords, such as a major third on top of a minor triad, can sound bizarre and are more common in jazz than pop or classical music. The seven chords we outlined should suffice for now. Feel free, however, to experiment with these chords. Just like we rearranged the table rows by moving the notes from left to right, we can rearrange these chords by moving the bottom note to the top of the chord. For example, instead of playing D, F, A, for a D minor triad, we could play F, A, D, or A, D, F. These rearrangements are called inversions because you are inverting the structure of the chord. The regular form of a chord is called ‘root position.’ Putting the bottom note on the top is called a first inversion. Putting the second note in addition to the first on the top is a second inversion. If you are playing a seventh chord, which has four notes, you can stack the third note on top to get a third inversion.

One Big Circle  |  Show

To conclude, we will see how everything comes together as one cohesive concept. Let’s redraw the table graphic that we used above to see the major and minor chord scales. Now we will examine the diagonal line of this table as we did with the other tables. Click ‘show’ to see the diagonal line. Do you recognize a familiar pattern? It’s the circle of fifths! If you’re confused, let’s reflect on how we have seen these table graphics behave. In Scales Part 1, we sliced off a chunk of neighboring notes from the circle of fifths. We extracted every other note from this chunk, which gave us the diatonic scale. Next, we selected every other note of the diatonic scale, which revealed the structure of chords. Now, we drew every other note of the structure of chords, and it formed the circle of fifths. It’s all one big circle!
