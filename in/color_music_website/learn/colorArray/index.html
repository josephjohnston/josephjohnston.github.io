Color Array

The meaning behind the colors.

A Distinct Pattern

The app features one primary color array called the ‘Circle of Fifths’ which the app will load by default. This primary color pattern is displayed on the keyboard to the right. From now on, any mention of a color array, pattern, arrangement, or scheme will refer to this Circle of Fifths color system utilized in the app. This color array serves as a meaningful tool for understanding musical harmony. These colors allow you to view any set of notes, and by examining their colors, you can sense how they will sound together, whether harmonious (smooth) or dissonant (rough). On this page we will discuss how we formulated this convenient arrangement of colors.

A Consistent Rearrangement

To apply any pattern to the piano keys, we must first look at a rearranged order of notes on the piano, called ‘chromatic’ order, illustrated by the ‘chromatic circle’ on the left. To demonstrate a rearrangement of this circle, we've drawn a star inside it that jumps in a consistent way around the circle to point to every note. If you follow the line, beginning on any note, you will see that every note is connected to every other note through this star.

A Harmonic Representation

After rearranging the notes of the chromatic circle according to the star, the notes appear in a new order pictured on the right. This new circle is called the ‘circle of fifths’ because it is composed of fifth intervals. Any two notes represented next to each other on the circle of fifths appear a fifth interval (seven notes) apart from each other on the piano. This rearrangement is spectacular because notes located near to one another on the circle of fifths sound harmonious when played together, while notes located further across the circle of fifths from each other sound more dissonant when played together. This means that if the physical keys of the piano were assembled in this circle of fifths order, you could easily identify the harmonic relationship between any two notes by simply considering their distance apart on the piano. Unfortunately, piano keys are not built that way.

Applying a Pattern

Our solution is to layer a linear pattern onto the order of notes in the circle of fifths and use stickers to display that pattern on your piano keys. This way, when you follow the linear pattern on the stickers, you will also follow the order of the circle of fifths. In other words, playing notes that are close to one other in the linear pattern of the stickers results in playing harmonious notes, while playing notes represented further apart from each other in the linear pattern of the stickers results in more dissonant notes. What linear pattern should we use to achieve this?

Applying Numbers

We could try applying the linear pattern of numbers one through twelve as shown below, and writing the numbers on the piano stickers. This pattern, however, does not naturally loop back on itself like the circle of fifths. Viewing these numbers on the piano, you would need to constantly jump from twelve back to one and vice versa.

Applying Colors

The linear pattern of the color spectrum is a logical one to layer onto the circle of fifths. This linear pattern naturally loops, and colors are easy to display on your piano keys with stickers. Below is an illustration of the color spectrum applied to the circle of fifths and then translated onto the piano keys. If you want to find harmonious note combinations, play piano keys displaying colors close to each other in the color spectrum (e.g. blue and green). If you want to play more dissonant harmony, play keys displaying colors on opposing sides of the color spectrum (e.g. yellow and purple). Study the pattern of these colors closely because every subsequent tutorial will utilize this color scheme in explaining musical concepts.
