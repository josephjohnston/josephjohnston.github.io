Harmony and Dissonance

An interval is the harmonic relationship between any two notes. Again, notes located near each other on the circle of fifths sound harmonious together, and notes located further apart on the circle sound more dissonant together. People might interpret harmonious combinations as sounding better than dissonant ones, but harmonious melodies can become monotonous. Dissonant combinations add kick – they have an edge to them that makes music so engaging.

Mathematical Relationship

There is also a mathematical explanation as to why some notes sound harmonious together while others sound dissonant. We will go into more detail on this through the remainder of the intervals section. Feel free to skip to Scales Part 1 if you prefer not to read this section. If you’re still here, pay close attention! Every note on the piano is attached to a string that is struck when you press a piano key. Each string is a different length, which renders a certain frequency wave (pitch) that we hear as the sound of that note. The mathematical relationship of an interval comes from measuring two aspects of that interval’s two notes. The first measurement concerns the relative string lengths of the two notes. The second measurement concerns the relative frequencies of the two notes, which are closely related to the string lengths.

Everything is Relative

In Part 2 of Intervals, we will address each interval according to this mathematical approach so that you can better understand why similar color intervals are harmonious and opposing color intervals are dissonant. In Part 2, we will compare note C to each of the other eleven notes. Below, C is represented by the longest string on the harp, which is currently darkened to exhibit it as the string of focus. When we discuss every note in relation to C, C is equal to one when measured relative to itself in both string length and frequency. The fraction on the left specifies relative string length while the fraction on the right specifies relative frequency. As we move through the intervals in Part 2, examine these fractions for each interval and see if you can detect patterns. As you can see, there are twelve strings to compare to C. Thus, we will discuss twelve intervals.
